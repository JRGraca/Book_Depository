                <div class="item-description">
                        <h2>Description</h2>
                        <div class="item-excerpt trunc" itemprop="description" data-height="230">

                            In this fascinating foray into the millennia-long relationship between science and military power, acclaimed astrophysicist and &#034;the world&#039;s best science communicator&#034; (Times Literary Supplement) Neil deGrasse Tyson and writer-researcher Avis Lang examine how the methods and tools of astrophysics have been enlisted in the service of war. &#034;The overlap is strong, and the knowledge flows in both directions&#034;, say the authors, because astrophysicists and military planners care about many of the same things: multi-spectral detection, ranging, tracking, imaging, high ground, nuclear fusion and access to space. Tyson and Lang call it a &#034;curiously complicit&#034; alliance. &#034;The universe is both the ultimate frontier and the highest of high grounds&#034;, they write. &#034;Shared by both space scientists and space warriors, it&#039;s a laboratory for one and a battlefield for the other. The explorer wants to understand it; the soldier wants to dominate it. But without the right technology-which is more or less the same technology for both parties-nobody can get to it, operate in it, scrutinise it, dominate it or use it to their advantage and someone else&#039;s disadvantage.&#034;<br />
                            <br />
                             Spanning early celestial navigation to satellite-enabled warfare, Accessory to War is a richly researched and provocative examination of the intersection of science, technology, industry and power that will introduce Tyson&#039;s millions of fans to yet another dimension of how the universe has shaped our lives and our world.<br />

                                <h2>About Neil Degrasse Tyson</h2>
                                <div class="block text-block trunc" data-height="100">

For half a decade, she edited Tyson&#039;s Natural History magazine column, Universe, parts of which became the basis for his Astrophysics for People in a Hurry, and later edited his anthology Space Chronicles.
